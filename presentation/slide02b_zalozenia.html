Design assumptions
What this deck assumes — and what I would clarify at the start of a real implementation
03 / 18 Scope & context
GiftFlow
Project assumption
Closed requirements scope

This presentation assumes that requirements have been gathered and are closed — the 5-step scenario and the 1000 PLN rule are the starting point for architecture.

In practice
Evolution and integration analysis

Before implementation I would add analysis from a growth perspective: new request types, additional thresholds, HR/ERP integrations, and a map of future flows — so architecture does not lock in after the first release.

Project assumption
Standalone system

The deck describes an autonomous solution (own backend, data, audit, analytics) — without tying it to a specific existing organizational landscape.

In practice
Fit to the IT ecosystem

I would align architecture with systems already in use, the maintenance team's skills, and the overall IT roadmap — e.g. low-code + pro-code hybrid, shared identities, and integration standards.

Project assumption
Ambitious technical approach

The deck evaluates a complex, pro-code solution (serverless, custom backend, data, analytics) — assuming a strong engineering team to build and maintain it.

In practice
Simplicity and off-the-shelf products

For something simpler — a Power Apps form from the Microsoft ecosystem. In many enterprises the standard is buying a ready-made market solution and implementing the full decision process there; meanwhile the GenAI revolution is changing the rules of the game and we can safely explore new ways of working.

GenAI and team decision: the current GenAI revolution may partially change these rules (BI copilot, categorization, automation). The final build vs buy vs low-code choice requires broader discussion within the technical team — this deck shows a pro-code reference variant, not the only valid path.
